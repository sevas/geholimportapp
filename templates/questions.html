Where do you get your data from and why should I trust the schedules I get from this service?

We fetch the data directly from the GeHoL service. The HTML content is then parsed using the popular BeautifulSoup python library. Events are then created and converted to iCalendar or CSV files. As far as we're concerned, these files are just another way to present the exact same information.

If you won't take our word for it, you can have a look at the source code of our underlying library on this bitbucket repository. You can even contribute, if you're into that sort of things.

The source code for the entire Google App Engine application is available on this bitbucket repository.

Both projects are released under the MIT Open Source License.

I clicked a link. I received some file. What do I do now?

We generate two kinds of files. CSV files can be imported in Google Calendar. iCalendar files can be imported in all major calendar applications.

By doing so, keep in mind that the files you download and import from this service are frozen snapshots taken from the GeHoL service. These files will contain schedules as they appear on GeHoL, at the time you clicked the download link. We hope you find it useful, but we won't be able to notify you of possible schedule changes if they occur. We have no technical mean to do so. To be notified of schedule changes, don't forget to check your official faculty announcements.

Can I add an iCalendar URL as a live feed?

TL;DR : Yes you can.

The files are generated on the fly, so you can copy the link and subscribe to it as a live feed, if your application supports it. By doing so, your calendar application will try to refresh and update the events periodically. It means that, if a schedule gets modified on GeHoL, the changes will appear automatically.

This has been tested with Google Calendar and Apple iCal. However, we have no control on how often your calendar application will try to refresh the events. We still strongly advise to check your official faculty announcements for that kind of information.
If no such communication channel exists, well, you should probably ask for one. Having to refresh a calendar view is a poor excuse for getting change notifications.

What's the deal with these weird, scrambled chessboards?

QR Codes are images that encode bits of information. In this case, internet addresses. The typical use-case is to take a picture of that image with your camera-enabled (smart)phone, using a dedicated application, and a web page should open up.

No shit. Just try it and have your mind blown. I'll wait.

Feels like the future, doesn't it?

In this service, every page with a schedule for a student profile will also display a QRCode which points to the mobile version of that page. From there, you will be able to quickly add the schedules in your native calendar application. To accomplish this, we use the webcal:// URL scheme, and as far as we know, it only works with iCal for iOS at the moment. Sorry, Android folks. Maybe you can embrace openness, learn git and contribute a patch or something.

Now, as entertaining as it is to open a page on your phone by taking a picture of the same page you just opened on your laptop, the usefulness is indeed questionable. This service really is aimed at professors, teaching assistants or anyone with teaching activities. Embed the code on your site. Print it and display it on the dead tree notification system. Yes, the one in your corridor. The hope is that it will give students a quicker access to their schedules.

Obviously, we're just trying out the idea. We'll see if it somehow catches up. Maybe we'll enhance it. Or Maybe we'll drop it.

Why did you do it?

If we look past the rather irritating visual presentation, one fundamental flaw of the GeHoL/Scientia® paradigm is the way information is presented.

To take one of the most extreme examples, we don't think anyone's problem is being solved by this kind of schedule (click on the image to witness it in its full glory):

Even with a simpler example, extracting useful information from each event box is tiresome and error-prone. Why should people have to convert an arbitrary week number into a real date ?

The purpose of a calendar system is to help you know when some important events occur. We think people should be able to integrate a schedule in their favorite calendar application. Providing an iCalendar file is the best way to achieve that.

What do you mean by "complete GeHoL URL" ?

This service can get you two types of schedules. The first one is per-course schedule. Using the course mnemonic, we can easily reconstruct the complete URL and fetch the data for you. The second type of schedules, however, is problematic, and we need a little work on your part to generate the iCalendar files.

You'll need to go here and select the values of interest as you normally would. The first two values are mandatory. The third one should be selected only if it makes sense to you. Something went wrong, what can I do?

We tried to test this service with many different sorts of schedules, from every faculty. However, parsing that kind of HTML is very fragile. We're not helped by the fact that the GeHoL/Scientia® service sometimes yields incomplete data. It would help if you could report unsuccessful queries to this address : geholimportapp@gmail.com.

The GeHoL server is down, now what?

We have no control over that server. Sometimes it gets disconnected from the internet with no indication of what is going on. We try at least to show it clearly in the sidebar on the right.
